Subtheme: Zoning

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. Is there a relationship between proximity to Boston and a higher percentage of single family homes?
    • I started thinking about this question because of one of the arguments used by many metro Boston municipalities to slow down desegregation during the Civil Rights Movement was to reduce the influx of residents from Boston to their more "relaxed" suburbs by restricting the multi-family housing available. As such, even though all of the municipalities we are analyzing are still somewhat close to Boston, I started wondering if the distance to Boston affected the percentage of land zoned in that municipality for single-family housing.
  2. How does the percentage of single family homes being constructed in the municipality affect the percentage of minority residents?
    • In a similar vein to my rationale for the previous question, I wondered if the racial motivations for increasing the amount of reserved land for single-family homes in the 60s extended to present day and whether those motivations remained confined to Black residents or extended to all minorities.
  3. How does the percentage of reserved single-family homes in a municipality affect the income distribution of its residents?
    • I expected this to be more of a "control" answer, since single-family homes are normally far more expensive than multi-family homes, and hence less affordable for lower-income residents. Hence, I expected to see a negative correlation between the percentage of land reserved for single-family housing and the percentage of lower-income households residing in the municipality. Of course, this ignores the presence of low-income affordable housing, but given that there are not many such units (as discussed in previous weeks), I figured that there would not be too much of an impact on the data. I hoped to use the results of this question's corresponding visualizations to inform how statistically significant the other results are.

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

I enjoyed learning about and exploring the impact of single-family zoning (a topic I did not know much about before this assignment) on various populations. My biggest takeaway (besides Tableau having a steep learning curve) is that the quality of your visualizations and results will only be as good as the completeness of your dataset. I used a combination of the two datasets provided for the zoning subtheme, and both had incomplete columns and/or data that lacked granularity, because of which, my results did not have the confidence that I expected them to have. I also found myself making many assumptions and taking several creative liberties (articulated throughout the captions) that underscored our discussion during reading group regarding the influence of the author's perspective and the subjectiveness of visualizations on the whole. Perhaps including certain data points or using different trend lines could have altered my results and how they are perceived. Finally, I realized that I still do not have a complete understanding of the issues surrounding zoning--in light of the results, my initial questions appeared too simplistic, but I am excited to keep learning more about this subtheme and others to form a more nuanced perspective.
